Learning Design

Nexus Insight

A self-paced professional-development resource for NYU faculty, built on a Google Site engineered to behave like an LMS: breadcrumbs, linear progression, and interactive decision-based scenarios instead of a wall of policy text.

The Problem
The Nexus wanted to give busy faculty structured, self-paced professional development they could finish on their own time, but without standing up a full LMS course for it. How do you deliver a real learning experience on the everyday tools you already have?
My Role
Graduate Student Learning Designer · NYU Learning & Teaching Nexus
The Outcome
Nexus Insights: asynchronous "Insights" built on a Google Site made to feel like a course shell, with branching scenarios authored in Genially. Two shipped (Supporting Student Success, Mayer's 12 Principles of Multimedia Learning). Live and faculty-facing.
Timeframe
2024–2025 · NYU Learning & Teaching Nexus

Context & Problem

The Nexus is NYU SPS's learning-and-teaching team. Part of its job is faculty development: helping instructors teach well, support students, and use the university's resources. The director wanted a particular kind of resource for that, something self-paced. Faculty are time-poor and teaching their own courses; they aren't going to sit through a workshop on a fixed schedule. They need to pull up a strategy, work through it on their own time, and apply it the next week.

The team named this format a Nexus Insight: an asynchronous resource that delivers key teaching strategies and concepts in a contained, finishable unit. The brief was essentially "make faculty development feel like a short, self-directed course."

The catch was the toolset. There was no dedicated LMS course shell to build this in; the practical tools on hand were Google Workspace. A plain Google Site, though, reads like a webpage, not a learning experience. You land somewhere, you scroll, you leave. There's no sense of progression, no "you are here," no path from start to finish. So the real design problem wasn't the content. It was structural: how do you make an ordinary Google Site behave like a learning platform, so that working through an Insight feels like moving through a course rather than browsing a site?

Process & Decisions

Make the Google Site impersonate an LMS. The first move was to borrow the structural cues that make an LMS legible and rebuild them by hand. Every Insight got a breadcrumb path across the top (Overview > 1. Student Success > 2. Scenarios > 3. Strategies > 4. Wrap-Up) so a learner always knows where they are in the sequence, and a linear Previous / Next control at the foot of every page so there's one obvious way forward. Content was broken into numbered, modular units. None of that is native to Google Sites; it's a template I designed so the site would feel like a course shell even though it's just web pages underneath.

Sequence for application, not just information. Each Insight is built as a deliberate arc rather than a stack of facts. Supporting Student Success moves: establish the stakes (as a faculty member, you're often the first to notice a student in trouble), then drop the learner into realistic dilemmas, then hand them a repeatable framework, then consolidate and point to where to get help. The teaching strategy is worked-example-to-application: see the situation, decide, then get the structure that generalizes.

Make it interactive, not passive. The Scenarios sit at the centre of that arc. Supporting Student Success was an existing Insight I was continuing; the scenario videos were already produced, and my contribution was building the interactive layer over them in Genially as branching, multi-select decision modules. Instead of reading "here is the absence policy," faculty watch a short scenario and choose how they'd respond, picking every option that applies. Four scenarios, each drawn from a real situation instructors face: students who are late or skipping class, troubling or sensitive content surfacing in an assignment, students struggling with the work itself, and a student who can't afford a required textbook. The point is to rehearse judgment, not memorize a handbook.

Ground it in evidence and existing support. The Strategies page distills a three-step framework, Observe, Converse, Refer, drawn from NYU's own guidance on engaging student mental health (Ragouzeos, 2023), and routes faculty to the real services that already exist (NYU Connect, the Student Support Center intake, the SPS Office of Student Affairs) so the resource connects to action rather than ending at advice.

My role across the two Insights was different in a way worth naming. On Supporting Student Success I was continuing existing work: the institutional content and scenario videos were already there, and I built the interactive Genially layer and the course-like structure around them, alongside the senior learning designers who own that material. Mayer's 12 Principles of Multimedia Learning went the other way. It started as my own graduate coursework on multimedia learning; the director saw it, liked it, and asked me to develop a custom version for Nexus. So the second Insight is content I authored and built end to end, from the principle breakdowns to both interactive panels, fitted into the same shell.

The Solution

Nexus Insights is a hub of self-paced Insights living on the NYU SPS Nexus Google Site. From a single homepage, faculty pick an Insight and hit "Start," and from there the breadcrumb-and-prev/next chrome carries them through it like a course.

The first, Supporting Student Success, runs across five pages. Student Success frames why faculty matter and surfaces the key resources. Scenarios puts them through the four interactive Genially cases. Strategies gives them the Observe, Converse, Refer framework, including a reference table of warning signs to watch for and concrete referral routes. Wrap-Up consolidates the message, points to a live workshop, slide deck and recording for anyone who wants to go deeper, and sends them back to the hub.

The second, Mayer's 12 Principles of Multimedia Learning, is the one I authored end to end, and it shows the shell carrying a very different kind of content. It runs Overview > 1. Universal Multimedia Principles > 2. Specific Multimedia Principles > 3. Wrap-Up. Rather than dumping all twelve principles in a list, I split them the way they actually apply: six universal principles that hold for any multimedia content, and six specific ones that only matter for particular media. Each set lives in its own interactive Genially panel, so faculty click through the principles one at a time with examples instead of reading a wall of theory, and the Wrap-Up frames them as a practical toolkit for building their own course materials and routes to Nexus support for going further. Same breadcrumb-and-prev/next shell, completely different subject, which is the real proof that the format is a reusable system and not a one-off page.

Outcomes & Impact

The deliverable is live and faculty-facing: two complete Insights running on the NYU SPS Nexus site, both built on one reusable template. That template is the quiet win. Because the structure, navigation, and interaction pattern are designed once and reused, the Nexus team can keep producing new Insights in the same shell without re-solving the "how do we make a Google Site feel like a course" problem each time.

It also reframed what an LMS actually gives you. Stripped to its essentials, much of an LMS's value is information architecture: knowing where you are, where you're going, and what to do next. Rebuilding those cues by hand showed how much of a "real" learning experience is structure rather than software.

Reflection

Reflection

Designing for faculty meant designing for a specific kind of learner: an expert, short on time, and reasonably allergic to being "trained." That pushed the work toward respect for the learner's time (contained, finishable units) and toward decisions over lectures (the Genially scenarios), rather than another long read they'd skim and forget.

The constraint turned out to be the best part. Not having an LMS forced a clearer information architecture than an LMS would have, because nothing was handled for me: every breadcrumb and Next button was a deliberate choice. What I'd want next is the thing the Google Site can't give: completion tracking and real usage data, so the Nexus could see which scenarios land, where faculty drop off, and whether the Observe, Converse, Refer framework actually changes what instructors do. For now, Nexus Insights proves the format works. Measuring it is the next Insight.
