What studying affective computing taught me about designing for real humans

Building a Tetris game that reads your face sounds like a gimmick. It turned into the most interesting design problem I've worked on, and made me rethink everything I assumed about what "learner-centered" actually means in practice.

This post is a placeholder for when I actually write it. The content will cover:

  • What affective computing actually is and why it's more interesting than it sounds
  • How the Affectris project started, and what the brief actually said
  • The moment it stopped being a technical challenge and became a design problem
  • What Rosalind Picard's work taught me about emotion, context, and the assumptions baked into "learner-centered" design
  • Why designing for emotional states is fundamentally different from designing for cognitive load
  • What I'd do differently now

This post is coming soon. In the meantime, you can read about the Affectris project itself in the case study.
